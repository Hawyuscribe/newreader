Sleep Neurology MCQ Q37

Conceptual Framework & Clinical Context

[Exam: Part 2 2020] Restless Legs Syndrome (RLS) is a sensorimotor disorder characterized by an irresistible urge to move the legs, usually accompanied by uncomfortable sensations. Symptoms worsen at rest and in the evening, improving with movement. Key pathophysiological factors include central dopaminergic dysfunction (particularly in the A11 hypothalamic nucleus and its spinal projections) and disrupted iron homeostasis in the substantia nigra. RLS is classified as primary (idiopathic, often with genetic predisposition) or secondary, where identifiable conditions—most commonly iron deficiency and pregnancy—precipitate symptoms. Recognizing secondary causes is essential for targeted management.

Evidence-Based Analysis

Pregnancy is a well-established risk factor for secondary RLS. Prospective cohort studies report a prevalence of 10–34% in the third trimester compared to ~5% in nonpregnant controls (Allen et al., Sleep Med Rev 2013). Iron demands increase by 1,000 mg during pregnancy, leading to relative deficiency and impaired dopamine synthesis. Hormonal shifts (elevated estrogen and progesterone) may further modulate dopaminergic and spinal inhibitory pathways. Most cases resolve within 4–6 weeks postpartum.

The American Academy of Sleep Medicine (AASM) Clinical Practice Guideline (2016) cites pregnancy as a Level A risk factor for secondary RLS and recommends checking serum ferritin, with supplementation if <75 ng/mL. A randomized trial of intravenous ferric carboxymaltose in iron-deficient pregnant women (Koch et al., Obstet Gynecol 2018) showed symptom reduction (p<0.01). No high-quality trials support a link between hypertension or COPD and RLS, and while diabetes mellitus–related neuropathy can mimic RLS sensations, diabetes itself does not independently confer the same risk magnitude.

Incorrect Options Analysis

B. Hypertension
– No robust epidemiological data link chronic hypertension to RLS prevalence. Hypertensive patients do not show increased RLS in large cross-sectional studies (Ondo et al., Mov Disord 2005). Misconception: equating vascular risk factors with neuropathic symptoms.

C. Diabetes mellitus
– Diabetes can cause peripheral neuropathy with paresthesias, but true RLS requires circadian worsening at rest and relief by movement. Meta-analyses show only a modest prevalence increase (~8%) in diabetics, far less than pregnancy. Students often conflate neuropathic pain with RLS.

D. Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease
– COPD lacks mechanistic or epidemiological association with RLS. Hypoxia does not trigger classic RLS features. Common error: assuming any discomfort at rest equals RLS.

Clinical Pearls

• RLS symptoms peak in the evening due to circadian dips in CNS iron and dopamine.
• Always measure ferritin; aim for >75 ng/mL before initiating dopaminergic therapy.
• Pregnancy-associated RLS often resolves postpartum—focus on nonpharmacologic measures and iron repletion.

Common Pitfalls and Misconceptions

1. Confusing diabetic peripheral neuropathy (constant, burning pain) with RLS (circadian, rest-induced, relieved by movement).
2. Overlooking transient causes—students may mislabel all RLS as idiopathic and miss reversible conditions like pregnancy or iron deficiency.
